# Facebook Style Guide

Updated: 2026-07-10
Reference page reviewed: `https://www.facebook.com/aobongdathietkeMAKAN`

## Purpose

This guide captures the posting style we want for `mayaobongro.vn` based on the
overall feel of the MAKAN page, then adapts it for basketball uniforms instead
of football kits.

Do not copy their wording or design 1:1. Reuse the structure, rhythm, and sales
logic.

## Core Style

The reference page sells with the image first, caption second.

Observed pattern:
- The post image already contains the main sales message.
- The image often looks like a mini landing page or sales sheet.
- Benefits are shown visually in short blocks, not buried in long text.
- Contact details and trust signals are repeated on the image itself.
- Captions usually open with a strong angle, not a generic greeting.
- Captions are emotional-commercial, but still easy to skim.

For our page, each post should feel like:
- a premium product showcase;
- a short custom-uniform sales pitch;
- a proof-of-quality post for teams, schools, clubs, and training groups.

## Visual Direction

### 1. Preferred image types

Rotate between these 3 formats:

1. Sales-sheet image
   Use a collage with:
   - hero jersey view;
   - 2-4 closeups;
   - short benefit labels;
   - hotline / website;
   - trust badges.

2. Model-wearing-uniform image set
   Use generated images with watermark when the pose, fit, or team identity is
   the main selling point.

3. Mixed proof set
   Combine:
   - full set front/back;
   - fabric or print closeup;
   - real outfit composition feel;
   - a short headline inside the image.

### 2. What should appear on-image

Whenever possible, put these directly in the visual:
- team or concept name;
- 3-5 product benefits;
- custom service promise;
- website or hotline;
- 1-3 trust markers.

Recommended benefit vocabulary:
- vai me the thao cao cap;
- in ten, so sac net, ben mau;
- duong may chac chan;
- co tron / co V thoai mai;
- cap quan co gian;
- thiet ke theo yeu cau;
- du size;
- bao hanh san pham.

### 3. Image tone

Aim for:
- bold contrast;
- premium sportswear feel;
- clear product focus;
- readable typography;
- organized composition.

Avoid:
- plain raw image dumps with no selling layer;
- too many tiny text blocks;
- soft pastel lifestyle look;
- messy mixed-color layouts with no focal point.

### 4. Facebook image-count rule

For normal Facebook feed posts, prefer:
- `4` images;
- `5` images when we need one extra variation or detail shot.

Avoid:
- `1` image when the visual has important text near the edges;
- `2` images when the crop makes the layout feel too narrow;
- `3` images for product-set posts, because Facebook often enlarges the first
  image and crops important content.

Reason:
- `4` or `5` images usually keep the grid more balanced;
- square visuals are more likely to stay readable;
- on-image hotline, logo, product name, and benefit labels are less likely to
  be cut awkwardly.

### 5. Image ordering rule

When posting a multi-image set:
- image 1 should still work if Facebook makes it the largest tile;
- place the safest square image first;
- avoid putting edge-heavy text on image 1;
- keep important text, logo, and contact details away from the outer crop zone;
- use images 2-5 for additional angles, closeups, alternate colorways, or
  front/back support views.

Recommended order:
1. safest hero square image;
2. strong alternate product view;
3. another clean model or full-set view;
4. support image with different color/design;
5. optional detail or extra variation.

### 6. Posting minimum for product showcases

For generated model posts, product showcases, and most sales posts:
- minimum `4` images;
- preferred `5` images if enough good square assets exist.

Only go below `4` when:
- the post is a special single-poster sales sheet;
- the post is a real update that does not rely on a square gallery layout;
- we explicitly accept the crop tradeoff.

## Caption Style

### 1. Caption role

The caption is not the whole sale. Its job is to:
- create the angle;
- explain who the set is for;
- reinforce quality and customization;
- push contact action softly.

### 2. Caption structure

Use this order most of the time:

1. Hook
   One strong opening line.

2. Short context
   Say who this set suits or what makes it stand out.

3. Benefit block
   Usually 3 short bullets or 1 short compact paragraph.

4. Custom service line
   Mention logo, color, number, team concept, or size range.

5. CTA
   Website + hotline/Zalo.

6. Hashtags
   Keep short and repeatable.

### 3. Tone

Target tone:
- direct;
- premium but not luxury-overdone;
- sales-aware;
- practical;
- easy to skim on mobile.

Good traits from the reference:
- opens with an idea, not filler;
- sounds like a brand that understands uniform making;
- keeps momentum;
- sells confidence and quality.

Avoid:
- over-cute phrasing;
- chatty Facebook diary tone;
- long generic motivational copy;
- keyword stuffing;
- too many emojis.

### 4. Pronoun rule

Use `ben em` consistently in captions and sales lines.

Avoid mixing:
- `ben minh`
- `ben toi`
- `shop`

For this page, `ben em` should be the default voice in:
- custom service lines;
- CTA lines;
- reassurance or trust-building lines.

## Recommended Post Mix

For a 10-day run, use a rhythm like:
- 4 adult-focused posts;
- 3 kids-focused posts;
- 2 mixed-age posts;
- 1 detail / craftsmanship post.

Alternate between:
- collage sales sheet;
- model showcase;
- story-led proof post.

## Basketball Adaptation Rules

When borrowing the MAKAN style, always convert the message to basketball-native
language:
- use `ao bong ro`, not football phrasing;
- talk about form when ra san, not just daily wear;
- mention club, school team, academy, training group, tournament use cases;
- highlight number, logo, color concept, and full-set coordination;
- keep the sportswear-premium feel, but with basketball identity.

## Practical Rules For Future Posting

Before posting, check:
- Does the image already sell on its own?
- Is the first line strong enough to stop scrolling?
- Is the audience clear: adult, kids, or mixed?
- Is there at least one quality or trust signal?
- Is the CTA present but not noisy?

If a post feels too plain, improve the image before making the caption longer.

## What We Should Not Copy

Do not copy:
- exact slogans;
- exact sentence openings;
- exact layout blocks;
- exact color system;
- exact trust-badge wording.

We are borrowing the system:
- visual-first selling;
- short commercial captioning;
- trust-driven product framing;
- repeated customization promise.
